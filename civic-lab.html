Civic Lab Services, Games & Blueprints

The Civic Lab is where the theory leaves the PDF. It’s a mix of consulting, workshops, and game‑style activities for student governments, clubs, classes, and youth projects that want to actually do democracy—not just talk about it.

Who the Civic Lab Is For

  • Student governments & MSL delegations: Need bylaws that actually work, not just copy‑paste from a random template.
  • Clubs & campus orgs: Want meetings that aren’t chaos and participation that isn’t just the same three people.
  • Educators & staff: Want plug‑and‑play activities that teach democratic skills without turning every class into a full simulation.
  • Youth projects & future delegates: Want missions and games that make civic life feel like something you can actually touch.

All work is nonpartisan and focused on democratic skills, structures, and habits—not pushing any particular party line.

Drop‑In Civic Lab Activities (Game‑Style)

These are structured activities you can run in 30–60 minutes. Think “lesson plan,” but with slightly more chaos and a lot more agency.

1. Agenda Jenga

Goal: Learn agenda‑setting and priorities without a three‑hour meeting.

You’ll need: Index cards or sticky notes, a whiteboard or wall.

  1. Everyone writes one real issue on a card (campus, club, community).
  2. Cards go on the board. As a group, you can only keep five.
  3. One person plays “chair” and leads a fast debate: what stays, what gets dropped, and why?
  4. Debrief: Who got heard? What issues dropped off first? Why?

Good for: Student gov retreats, club officer trainings, youth leadership programs.

2. Budget Lab: Slice the Pie

Goal: Practice trade‑offs and coalition‑building.

You’ll need: A fake budget (or your real one), categories, markers.

  1. Give groups a simple “budget pie”: e.g., 100 points to divide among 6 categories.
  2. Each group must create a budget and justify it in 2–3 bullet points.
  3. Groups present; then a second round happens after hearing others’ arguments.
  4. Compare Round 1 vs Round 2: What shifted? Did anyone change their mind? What arguments moved people?

Good for: SGA/club funding discussions, civic classes, MSL training.

3. Parli Pro Speedrun

Goal: Make parliamentary procedure less terrifying and more like a co‑op game.

You’ll need: A short cheat sheet of motions and a silly sample issue.

  1. Pick a low‑stakes topic (snacks budget, club trip, theme for an event).
  2. Assign roles: chair, clerk, timekeeper, 4–6 members. Everyone gets a quick motions cheat sheet.
  3. Run a 10–15 minute mock meeting where the only goal is: use at least 3 different motions correctly (e.g., move to amend, move to table, call the question).
  4. Debrief: What felt empowering? What felt confusing or gatekeeping‑ish?

Good for: MSL prep, student government training, intro to Robert’s Rules.

4. Spectacle vs Substance

Goal: Train students to separate political “show” from real decisions.

You’ll need: A few screenshots or clips of political content (TV, social, campus emails).

  1. Show a clip or read a short excerpt.
  2. Ask: “What’s the actual decision here, if any?” Write it down.
  3. Ask: “Who decides? Where? When?” Map it onto local/state/national or campus.
  4. Brainstorm one concrete action a student could take that relates to the real decision.

Good for: Youth workshops, media literacy sessions, first‑year seminars.

AI Helpers for Civic Work (Used Safely)

AI is not a replacement for real civic work, but it can be a surprisingly useful assistant if you tell it exactly what you want and force it to show its sources. The Civic Lab uses AI in a few focused ways:

Ask the Assembly (Maryland Legislative Help)

You can use an AI assistant (like ChatGPT) alongside the official Maryland General Assembly resources to get plain‑language explanations. A safe pattern:

  1. Go to the Maryland General Assembly website and find the relevant page (e.g., “How a Bill Becomes Law,” rules, committee info).
  2. Copy the relevant text or link and paste it into your AI chat with a prompt like:
    “Using only this official Maryland General Assembly material, explain in simple terms how a bill moves from introduction to becoming law. Flag any steps that are unique to Maryland.”
  3. Ask follow‑up questions, but always cross‑check with the official site before using the answer in anything formal.

Rule: AI helps you understand; the Assembly is still the authority.

Bylaw Builder & Rule Rewrite

For clubs and delegations, AI can help refactor messy documents if you keep it on a tight leash:

  • Paste your constitution/bylaws into a document.
  • Ask an AI: “Rewrite this into a clear, student‑friendly bylaws document, but do not change any powers or voting thresholds. Just clarify and reorganize.”
  • Review the output line by line before adopting anything.

Agenda & Email Draft Assist

You can also use AI to generate sample agendas, recruitment emails, and training outlines. The Lab can help you design the prompts so you get useful drafts instead of generic spam.

Note: A future version of this site may include a dedicated “Civic Lab Assistant” that sits on top of official manuals and this project’s research. For now, the Lab offers guidance on how to use AI well; the tools you use (ChatGPT, etc.) are up to you and your institution’s policies.

Service Packages & Collaborations

Starter Civic Blueprint Package

Ideal for new student governments, MSL delegations, or youth councils trying to get real structure without losing their soul.

  • A customized constitution & bylaws template tailored to your campus or club’s needs.
  • Sample agendas, recruitment scripts, and communications to kick‑start engagement.
  • One 60‑minute virtual consultation to review your structure and answer questions.
  • A follow‑up implementation guide with tips on elections, budgeting, and member training.

Pricing: Sliding‑scale, depending on organization size and scope. Reach out to discuss.

Custom Consulting & Workshops

For established organizations or institutions seeking deeper support, the Civic Lab offers tailored services such as:

  • Facilitated workshops on democratic skills (agenda‑setting, deliberation, consensus‑building, parliamentary procedure).
  • One‑on‑one advising for leaders on strategic planning, engagement initiatives, and “how not to burn out your best people.”
  • Curriculum co‑design for mock legislatures, civic labs, and first‑year civic seminars.
  • Help integrating AI responsibly into civic education (prompt design, guardrails, and source‑checking methods).

Pricing: Custom proposals; designed to be realistic for student org budgets and small institutions.

Call to Action: What You Can Do Right Now

  • Student leader? Pick one activity above (Agenda Jenga, Budget Lab, Parli Pro Speedrun) and run it at your next meeting.
  • Club or delegation? Start a shared doc titled “Our Civic Blueprint” and list what’s working, what’s broken, and what you want help with.
  • Instructor? Grab one activity and drop it into a 30‑minute block in your syllabus as a live experiment.
  • Youth group or teacher? Pair this page with the Civic Engagement Project missions to create a mini civic unit.

If you try something and it works (or crashes), that’s data. The Lab is built on those experiments.
