Research Questions & Hypotheses

The study asks how universities can cultivate a culture of democratic engagement that reaches all students, including those who feel politically out of place. Drawing on work on civic organizations and “cross-pressured” members, the central claim is that organizational communication and norms can either widen or close the gap between politically comfortable and politically misaligned students.

The main research question is:

How do different ways of framing campus civic engagement, combined with norms about participation, shape democratic engagement among college students—especially those who perceive themselves as politically misaligned with the campus climate?

From this, we derive three core hypotheses:

  • H1 (Framing): Messages that frame civic engagement around shared campus identity and local issues will increase students’ sense of representation and willingness to participate, relative to a neutral control and a national partisan conflict frame.
  • H2 (Norms): Messages that highlight descriptive norms—that many students participate in some form of civic activity—will further boost engagement by signaling that participation is expected and socially supported.
  • H3 (Cross-pressure moderation): The positive effects of identity- and norm-based frames will be strongest for students who perceive themselves as politically misaligned with the campus climate (“cross-pressured” students).

Design Overview

The project uses a mixed-methods approach anchored by a campus-based survey experiment. The design has two linked goals:

  1. Substantive: Test whether framing and norms can move key outcomes like political efficacy, perceived campus representation, and willingness to engage.
  2. Design/developmental: Generate effect-size estimates, refine measures, and identify implementation challenges in preparation for a larger, multi-campus study.

The pilot experiment is embedded in an online survey administered to students at a liberal arts college. Participants are randomly assigned to one of four short messages about campus civic engagement (described below), then answer questions and face a real, low-stakes opportunity to engage.

Population & Sampling

The immediate population of interest is undergraduate students enrolled at a small, liberal arts college. In practice, the pilot draws on:

  • Students in introductory political science and general education courses.
  • Students reached through a campus-wide email invitation.
  • Students contacted via student government, civic organizations, and relevant non-partisan clubs (e.g., service, debate, MSL delegation).

The target sample size for the pilot is approximately 150–250 respondents. This is sufficient to:

  • Detect moderate treatment effects on attitudinal outcomes with basic regression models.
  • Produce preliminary effect-size estimates for interaction effects to inform later power analyses.

Participation is voluntary; students may opt in via survey links distributed through classes, email, and organization channels. No single class is required to participate, and participation is not tied to course grades.

Key Measures

The full item wording appears on the Questionnaires page; here we summarize the main indices.

Background & Demographics

  • Year in school, major/minor, residence (on/off campus).
  • Race/ethnicity, gender, first-generation college status.
  • Past political behavior (voting where eligible, campaign activities, etc.).

Political Identity & Cross-Pressure

  • Ideology: liberal–conservative 7-point scale.
  • Partisanship: party ID with leaners and non-identifiers.
  • Perceived campus climate: perception of “typical politically active student.”
  • Misfit index: “out of place” / “views not represented” scale.

Democratic Engagement Outcomes

  • Internal efficacy (campus-specific): “People like me can influence decisions here.”
  • External efficacy/trust: “Decision-makers take student input seriously.”
  • Engagement intentions: voting intent + willingness to join civic actions.
  • Behavioral outcome: real sign-up / click-through opportunity post-treatment.

Experimental Treatments

Participants are randomly assigned one of four short messages. The longer-form message text is documented on the Dissertation page.

Condition 1: Neutral Control

Benign campus info; no civic appeal; no norms language.

Condition 2: Partisan Conflict Frame

National polarization framing; “fight for your side” logic.

Condition 3: Campus Identity & Local Stakes Frame

Shared campus identity + tangible local stakes; de-emphasizes partisan identity.

Condition 4: Identity + Norms Frame

Condition 3 + descriptive norms: participation is typical/expected.

Analytic Strategy

1. Balance & Randomization Checks

Descriptive checks confirm covariate balance across conditions (ideology, misfit index, key demographics).

2. Main Treatment Effects

For continuous outcomes, estimate OLS models:

Yᵢ = β₀ + β₁T₂ᵢ + β₂T₃ᵢ + β₃T₄ᵢ + Xᵢγ + εᵢ

For binary outcomes (e.g., sign-up), logistic regression or LPMs with robustness checks.

3. Cross-Pressure Interactions

Yᵢ = β₀ + β₁T₂ᵢ + β₂T₃ᵢ + β₃T₄ᵢ
      + β₄CrossPressureᵢ
      + β₅(T₃ᵢ × CrossPressureᵢ)
      + β₆(T₄ᵢ × CrossPressureᵢ)
      + Xᵢγ + εᵢ

4. Pilot Orientation

  • Effect sizes + uncertainty emphasized over “definitive” claims.
  • Sensitivity checks for inattentive responses / alt codings.
  • Identify what must change before multi-campus scale-up.

Implementation Notes & Limitations

  • Single-campus context: external validity requires multi-campus replication.
  • Self-selection: participation may skew civic-interested; compare to campus demographics where possible.
  • Short-term outcomes: longer-term civic behaviors need panel or admin linkages.

Ethics, Consent, & Data Protection

Any real implementation will require IRB review (or equivalent). Core principles:

  • Voluntary participation with no penalties for non-participation.
  • Informed consent up front; minimal-risk framing; clear data use statement.
  • Confidentiality: identifiers separated (if collected at all) from survey responses.
  • Right to withdraw at any time.
  • Debriefing at the end explaining message variations + civic resources.

Questions about ethics or data use should be directed to the project owner via the Main Site (or to the relevant IRB office at the participating institution).

ALTIUS Prototype: Role in the Civic Engagement Experiment

ALTIUS is an experimental, AI-aligned task engine developed inside the Civic Engagement Experiment & Lab. Rather than answering questions in place of the user, ALTIUS is designed to reduce inertia and help students move from abstract intention (“I should get involved” or “I should write this paper”) toward concrete action.

In research terms, ALTIUS functions as a guided intervention layer. It does not replace the campus survey experiment; it complements it by generating structured process data on how people actually try to solve tasks when supported by an agency-first co-agent.

Core Design Questions

  • When does an AI task engine increase agency vs. dependence?
  • Which prompt structures reliably convert intent into follow-through?
  • Can task-completion patterns illuminate civic disengagement behaviors (avoidance, fatigue, delay)?

Interaction Pattern & Task Engine Logic

  1. Clarify: name the avoided task.
  2. Decompose: break into atomic steps.
  3. Anchor: choose the smallest non-zero action.
  4. Execute & Reflect: do it, report back, adapt.

Data, Ethics, & Guardrails

  • Agency-first: no partisan pushing; no decision-making for users.
  • Data minimization: future research use relies on anonymized aggregates.
  • Transparency: clear consent framing; easy opt-out.
  • Non-therapeutic scope: not a mental health tool; refer out when needed.
