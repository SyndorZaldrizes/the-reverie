Instruments & Templates

Questionnaires & Surveys

This page collects the survey instruments used in The Reverie’s campus study: student questionnaires, faculty surveys, and reusable design templates.

Status: Draft → Pilot-testing → IRB-ready deployment (when applicable). · Last updated: Dec 2025

How These Questionnaires Fit into the Study

The Reverie is built around a campus-level study of democratic engagement. The questionnaires here are the tools that make the study possible. They:

  • Measure how students and faculty experience politics on campus.
  • Capture cross-pressure and feelings of “political misfit.”
  • Track attitudes like political efficacy, trust, and willingness to engage.
  • Provide clean, reusable templates for future experiments and campuses.

For the methodological backbone behind these instruments, see Study Details & Research Design and the Dissertation Project page.

Current & Pilot Surveys

At this stage, the project is in a pilot / development phase. That means instruments are being tested, refined, and aligned with ethics requirements before broader rollout.

When a live survey opens to participants, it will appear here with a clearly labeled link and a short description of who it is for and what it measures.

Right now: no public-facing surveys are open. Pilot testing is done in controlled settings with appropriate consent.

Student Civic Engagement Questionnaire

Instrument status: Draft · Pilot-testing (controlled)

The student questionnaire is the main instrument for the campus experiment. It captures:

  • Background: year, major, first-generation status, race/ethnicity, gender, residence.
  • Political identity & cross-pressure: ideology, partisanship, perceived campus climate, and “fit” (how comfortable students feel in campus political conversations).
  • Efficacy & trust: campus-specific measures of internal and external political efficacy and trust in decision-makers.
  • Engagement: intentions and behavior around voting, forums, contacting officials, and participating in campus civic events.
  • Experimental items: outcome questions following the framing / norms treatments described in the study design.

The questionnaire is maintained in spreadsheet and CSV form so it can be imported into survey platforms (Qualtrics, Google Forms, etc.) or used directly in classroom exercises.

Faculty & Instructor Questionnaire

Instrument status: Draft · Pilot-testing (limited outreach)

In addition to student responses, the project includes a short faculty/instructor questionnaire. This instrument focuses on:

  • Classroom context: whether and how civic topics appear in courses.
  • Faculty perceptions: how instructors perceive student engagement, hesitation, or cross-pressure in their classes.
  • Support & constraints: what resources or institutional support faculty feel they have (or lack) for fostering discussion and participation.

The faculty survey is designed to be short enough to respect time constraints while still offering a parallel perspective on the same campus dynamics the student survey measures.

Using These Instruments on Your Campus

If you are an instructor, student leader, or civic organization thinking, “Could we run a version of this?”—yes, but with care.

Before deploying these questionnaires outside a class exercise, you’ll want to:

  • Check ethics/IRB: Make sure any data collection meets your institution’s human subjects rules.
  • Adapt carefully: Keep core items consistent if you want comparisons across campuses, but add local-specific items where needed.
  • Plan analysis up front: Decide what you need to learn (cross-pressure patterns, engagement gaps, framing effects) before collecting data.

If you want help customizing these instruments for a club, delegation, or class, that’s what the Civic Lab side of this project is for.

Privacy, Consent & Data Handling

Any live survey associated with this project will include a clear consent statement at the beginning. Participation is voluntary, and responses are kept confidential or anonymized according to whatever the approved protocol requires.

In brief:

  • No grades or evaluations are tied to participation.
  • Identifiable information (if collected) is stored separately from survey data.
  • Aggregate results may be used in academic work, presentations, or reports without naming individual participants.

For full details on ethics and data protection, see the Ethics, Consent & Data Protection section on the Study Details page.
