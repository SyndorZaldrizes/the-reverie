Who this is for

Student governments & delegations

Educators & youth programs

Drop-in activities (game-style)

These are structured “serious games” you can run in 30–60 minutes. Minimal prep. Maximum signal.

1) Agenda Jenga

Goal: learn agenda-setting + priorities without a three-hour meeting.

You’ll need: index cards or sticky notes; a wall/board.

  1. Everyone writes one real issue on a card (campus, club, community).
  2. Cards go up. As a group, you can only keep five.
  3. One person plays “chair” and runs a fast debate: what stays, what drops, why.
  4. Debrief: who got heard, what got cut first, what the power dynamics were.

Good for: retreats, officer training, youth leadership programs.

2) Budget Lab: Slice the Pie

Goal: practice trade-offs and coalition-building.

You’ll need: a fake budget (or your real one), categories, markers.

  1. Give each group a “pie”: 100 points across 6 categories.
  2. Groups allocate + justify in 2–3 bullet points.
  3. Present; then run a second round after hearing other arguments.
  4. Compare Round 1 vs Round 2: what shifted, and why.

Good for: funding debates, civic classes, delegation training.

3) Parli Pro Speedrun

Goal: turn parliamentary procedure into a co-op skill, not a gatekeeping ritual.

You’ll need: a tiny motions cheat-sheet + a low-stakes topic.

  1. Pick a harmless agenda item (snacks budget, event theme, trip vote).
  2. Assign roles: chair, clerk, timekeeper, 4–6 members.
  3. Run a 10–15 min mock meeting: use at least 3 motions correctly.
  4. Debrief: what felt empowering vs confusing, and why.

Good for: Robert’s Rules intros, MSL prep, org leadership training.

4) Spectacle vs Substance

Goal: teach students to separate political “show” from real decision points.

You’ll need: a few clips/screenshots (TV, social, campus emails).

  1. Show the content.
  2. Ask: “What’s the actual decision here?” Write it down.
  3. Ask: “Who decides, where, and when?” Map the path.
  4. Brainstorm one concrete action tied to the decision.

Good for: media literacy sessions, first-year seminars, youth workshops.

AI helpers for civic work (used safely)

AI isn’t civic engagement. But it can assist—if you force it to be specific, grounded, and source-aware.

What AI is allowed to do here

  • Clarify complicated procedures in plain language (then you verify).
  • Draft agendas, emails, workshop outlines (you edit + own it).
  • Refactor messy bylaws without changing powers or thresholds (you review line-by-line).
  • Help you create “next step” plans—especially when people freeze.

What AI is not allowed to be

  • A substitute for institutional authority (laws, official manuals, policy).
  • A citation machine without verification.
  • A “do my civic work for me” button (outsourcing agency kills the point).
  • A black box that demands trust. If it can’t explain, it doesn’t get to decide.
