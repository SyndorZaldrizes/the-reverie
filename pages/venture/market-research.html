Market & Research

Before we build “solutions,” we check whether anyone actually needs them—and why they aren’t already satisfied. This page is the anti-delusion layer: user segments, friction patterns, and the competitor map.

Market insights (early)

This is less about spreadsheets and more about patterns: where demand is real, where it’s fake, where the gaps are, and which delusions die the moment you talk to actual humans.

Core thesis: Most civic work fails because it assumes people have time, patience, and context. They don’t. So the product has to work under scarcity: attention scarcity, time scarcity, motivation scarcity.

Potential user segments

The initial hypothesis was “everyone who cares about democracy will want this.” Reality: the interested groups are specific—and they’re allergic to bloat.

  • Student governments & delegations: need clear procedures + fast onboarding.
  • Campus clubs & societies: want to avoid the “tyranny of the few” and spread decision-making.
  • Educators & staff: want plug-in activities that fit a 30-minute block.
  • Youth programs & after-school groups: want engaging civics that doesn’t feel like homework.
  • Small nonprofits & civic orgs: need lightweight tools without enterprise pricing or IT overhead.

Reality check: if the offering requires “read this 30-page PDF first,” it’s dead on arrival.

What we learned (so far)

Talking to people produced the same themes over and over. The themes are annoying—because they’re true.

  • People are time-poor. If it can’t work inside a meeting, it doesn’t exist.
  • Nobody reads PDFs. Interactive guides beat static documents, every time.
  • Training is the bottleneck. Clubs reinvent the wheel annually because memory is fragile.
  • “AI” is a buzzword. People want help, not replacement; transparency and control matter.
  • Cost sensitivity is real. Student orgs and small nonprofits run on fumes.

Implication: the real product might be onboarding + meeting execution + structured follow-through, not “a civic platform™”.

Competitor landscape (rough map)

The space is a weird mix of polished civic tech platforms and improvised Google Docs. We’re not cloning what exists—we’re aiming at the gaps between categories.

Category Examples Notes
Civic education games iCivics, classroom democracy kits Great for K–12; less useful for real campus governance or meeting execution.
Governance platforms Decidim, Loomio Robust but heavy; often require IT support, budgets, and organizational maturity.
Parliamentary procedure guides Robert’s Rules cheat sheets Useful reference, but no interactive practice or “in-meeting” tooling.
AI productivity tools ChatGPT, Notion AI General-purpose; not civic-context aware; encourages dependence if unmanaged.

Our niche is somewhere between: “teach me how to govern” and “help me run this meeting and capture decisions cleanly”.

Research roadmap

The research is ongoing: deeper dives into non-college youth programs, pilot deployments on campuses, and partnerships with civic tech providers who don’t mind a little experimentation.

  • User discovery: 10–20 short interviews across each segment.
  • Pilot deployments: one campus org + one classroom + one staff office.
  • Measure outcomes: onboarding speed, retention, meeting quality, follow-through rate.
  • Publish notes: what worked, what didn’t, what changed.

Collaboration: If you have data, anecdotes, or cynical hot takes, the lab always wants them. (We deleted Team & Contact, so routing will live elsewhere later—no dead links here.)
