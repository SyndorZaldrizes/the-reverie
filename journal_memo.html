Civic Organizations and Democratic Engagement

About Study Design Participate Contact Journal Memo

Thesis

Democratic engagement is not enhanced merely by exhorting individuals to care about politics. Instead, it is cultivated within organizations that purposefully build inclusive norms of participation and connect civic action to shared, non‑partisan identities. In polarized environments, civic organizations—including unions and campus groups—can mobilize even ideologically dissimilar members by emphasizing local stakes and framing participation as part of belonging.

Why This Article?

This memo analyzes Alexander Hertel‑Fernandez’s 2024 article “Civic Organizations and the Political Participation of Cross‑Pressured Americans: The Case of the Labor Movement,” published in the American Political Science Review. The piece examines how local unions engage conservative teachers in a mostly liberal labor movement. Its focus on “cross‑pressured” members—individuals whose ideological views differ from their organization’s dominant stance—mirrors the challenge of engaging politically diverse students on a college campus. The study meets the criteria of an empirical, post‑2015 research article in a top political science journal.

Author’s Argument

Hertel‑Fernandez argues that civic organizations can successfully mobilize cross‑pressured members when leaders cultivate a strong internal culture of participation. In the Iowa teachers’ union he studies, locals that stress participation as a norm, frame political work around members’ shared identity as educators and connect issues to local concerns see higher engagement among conservative members. Rather than being demobilized by disagreement, cross‑pressured members respond positively when organizations emphasize belonging and local relevance.

Evidence Provided

The article employs a mixed‑methods design. Original surveys of union members and leaders measure political attitudes, union culture and participation. By matching members to their specific local leaders, the study assesses whether variations in leadership practices correspond to differences in engagement among conservative members. Supplemental survey data from the National Education Association and interviews with local leaders bolster the findings. Regression models show that locals with stronger participatory norms have higher engagement among conservative teachers, while interviews reveal how leaders frame political action around identity and local stakes.

Assessment of Evidence

The evidence is persuasive and appropriate to the research question. The matched survey design links organizational practices to individual behavior, and the combination of quantitative and qualitative data illuminates the mechanisms at work. While the study is confined to one state and one type of organization, its findings suggest that organizational culture can overcome ideological misalignment. Future research should test these mechanisms across different organizations and contexts.

Follow‑Up Study

To extend this work, a pilot experiment on a college campus can test whether framing civic engagement around shared campus identity, local issues and participation norms increases engagement among cross‑pressured students. The study would randomly assign students to receive different messages (neutral, partisan conflict, identity + local stakes and identity + local stakes + norms) and measure attitudes and behavior. Evidence from the pilot could justify a multi‑campus field experiment and inform practices for civic organizations beyond unions.
