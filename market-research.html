Market & Research Insights

Before you can build something useful, you have to know who might actually use it and why they aren’t already satisfied. This page summarizes the early market research and user interviews guiding the Civic Lab’s venture track. It’s less about spreadsheets and more about patterns: where the demand is, where the gaps are, and which delusions we had to abandon after talking to actual humans.

Potential User Segments

Our initial hypothesis was “everyone who cares about democracy will want this.” Reality is more specific. These are the groups who responded with curiosity rather than glazed eyes:

  • Student governments & delegations: Need clear procedures and ways to train new members quickly.
  • Campus clubs & societies: Want to avoid the “tyranny of the few” and spread decision‑making beyond officers.
  • Educators & staff: Looking for pre‑built activities that fit into a 30‑minute class block without derailing the syllabus.
  • Youth programs & after‑school groups: Crave engaging content that doesn’t feel like recycled civics homework.
  • Small nonprofits & civic orgs: Need lightweight tools to run deliberative meetings and capture member input without paying enterprise prices.

What We Learned So Far

Talking to people (rather than talking to ourselves) produced a few recurring themes:

  • People are time‑poor. Anything that takes longer than a meeting gets pushed aside; prototypes must work in minutes, not hours.
  • Nobody reads PDFs. Even earnest leaders admitted they rarely read their own constitutions; interactive guides beat static documents.
  • Training is a bottleneck. Clubs reinvent the wheel each year because there’s no institutional memory; onboarding tools are valued more than high‑concept theory.
  • “AI” is a buzzword. Users are intrigued by AI helpers but are wary of outsourcing decisions; they want transparency and control.
  • Cost sensitivity is real. Student orgs and small nonprofits operate on shoestring budgets; freemium models or institutional sponsorships are essential.

Competitor Landscape

The space is a strange mix of polished civic tech platforms and home‑grown Google Docs. Our goal isn’t to clone what already exists, but to slot into the gaps. Here’s a rough map:

Category Examples Notes
Civic education games iCivics, Classroom Democracy kits Great for K–12; less helpful for real campus politics
Governance platforms Decidim, Loomio Robust but heavy; often require IT support and budgets
Parliamentary procedure guides Robert’s Rules cheat sheets Useful reference, but no interactive practice
AI productivity tools ChatGPT, Notion AI General‑purpose; not tailored to civic contexts

We respect these tools and occasionally borrow from them. The Civic Lab’s niche is somewhere between “teach me how to govern” and “help me actually run this meeting.”

Research Roadmap

The research is ongoing. Upcoming steps include deeper dives into non‑college youth programs, pilot deployments on campuses, and partnerships with civic tech providers who don’t mind a little experimentation. If you have data, anecdotes or cynical hot takes to share, we’d love to hear them.
