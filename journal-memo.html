Journal Memo & Dissertation Proposal

This page presents a polished journal memo that lays the groundwork for my senior dissertation on democracy and civic engagement. The memo synthesizes key literature, proposes a research question, and outlines the methodological approach I plan to use. It builds on reading notes and classroom discussions and serves as an example of the more formal writing that will live on this site.

Introduction

Democratic erosion has become one of the defining concerns of contemporary political science. While much of the scholarship focuses on institutions, leaders, and macro‑level indicators, this memo argues that the roots of democratic resilience or decay can be found in the everyday beliefs and practices of citizens. Borrowing from Danielle Allen’s emphasis on civic friendship and Elizabeth Cohen’s work on democratic deficit, I suggest that understanding how people learn about democracy—before and beyond formal instruction—is crucial for explaining why some communities defend democratic norms while others retreat into cynicism.

Research Question

The central question of this project is:

How do individuals’ early experiences with political participation and civic discourse shape their later commitment to democratic norms?

In other words, can we trace a link between the way young people encounter politics—through school, family conversations, social media, or organizing—and their willingness to defend or abandon democratic procedures when those procedures are threatened?

Theoretical Framework

This memo draws on a range of theoretical traditions. From normative democratic theory, it incorporates Hannah Arendt’s insistence that politics should be a space of public freedom rather than private satisfaction. From empirical studies, it engages with research on political socialization and civic education, particularly work showing that early political discussions at home and in classrooms have long‑term effects on participation.

Critical perspectives like those of Charles Mills remind us that not everyone is invited into the democratic conversation, and that what counts as “participation” is shaped by race, class, and gender. This proposal therefore situates individual learning within broader structures of power.

Methodology

I propose a mixed‑methods study that combines qualitative interviews with quantitative survey data. Semi‑structured interviews will be conducted with college students who have participated in legislative simulations (like the Maryland Student Legislature) and those who have not. Surveys will measure participants’ political efficacy, tolerance for dissent, and understanding of democratic procedures. The goal is to see whether early exposure to democratic practices (beyond voting) correlates with stronger commitments to norms like fair play and peaceful turnover of power.

Expected Contributions

This project aims to contribute to both democratic theory and civic education practice. Theoretically, it bridges micro‑level socialization research with macro‑level democratic erosion debates. Practically, it offers insight into how educators and organizers might foster more durable democratic commitments by designing experiences that model inclusive, deliberative politics at an early age.

Conclusion

Democracy is not self‑sustaining. It relies on people who have learned to see themselves as part of a collective project, capable of both speaking up and listening. By exploring the pathways through which individuals come to value democratic norms, this research seeks to illuminate how that commitment can be renewed in an era of polarization and disinformation.
